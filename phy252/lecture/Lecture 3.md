Imagine you have a coin at room temperature. If you pick up this coin, you would expect that it is a little cold. But, there is gas bumping into you constantly, gas at the same temperature of this coin. So, why does the coin feel colder than the air? Well, it's because when humans touch things, we mostly sense how much heat is "flowing" from us to what we are in contact with. There isn't a lot of heat going from us into the air. But we begin transferring a lot of energy into the coin.

*Thermal reservoirs* are objects with a very large heat capacity such that $C_\text{reservoir}\gg C_\text{obj}$. This way, when some object comes into contact with it, the temperature of the reservoir hardly changes, but we can change the temperature of the object.
- Idealized object, doesn't really exist. But we can have some approximations.

For a thermometer, we would like it to have a small heat capacity. This is why they are both physically small, and have a low specific heat capacity. This way, it changes quickly when it comes in contact with whatever we are trying to measure.
- Furthermore, if the heat capacity of a thermometer is too high, once it comes into thermal contact with the object you are trying to measure, it will actually change the temperature of that object!

Generally speaking, depending on the energy scales of the problem, it must be tackled differently. We cannot use a basic thermodynamic understanding of problems for states that are substantially cold, or substantially hot.
