# Question 1
---
a.
The potential energy is:
$$
	\Delta U = mgh
$$
Where $h$ in this case is the length of the pendulum $l$. Once it reaches the bottom, all the energy is kinetic energy. And so the kinetic energy is:
$$
	\Delta KE = mgl
$$
---
b.
Potential energy is converted into kinetic energy,
$$
	\frac{1}{2} mv^{2} = mgh \implies \frac{1}{2} v^{2} = gh
$$
$$
	v=\sqrt{ 2gh }
$$
Assuming all energy is conserved, it should bounce all the way back up.

---
c.
Work energy theorem states,
$$
	W = \Delta KE
$$
However, assuming something bounces off a wall elastically, the wall does not need to move, doing no work. Therefore, no work is required to reverse the movement of a particle.
# Question 2
---
a.
The pressure in a liquid of uniform density is:
$$
	p=\rho gh
$$
Where $\rho$ is the density of the liquid, and $h$ is the depth in the liquid. Therefore,
$$
	p=\rho gh \implies h = \frac{p}{\rho g} = \frac{100 \times 10^{3}}{1 \times 9.8} \approx 10204.08
$$
So 10k metres, or 10.204 kilometres.
# Question 3
---
a.
Imagine we have a system with a spring with constant $k$ and mass $m$. The units for the spring constant is,
$$
	k \propto \frac{m}{t^{2}}
$$
Where $m$ is mass and $t$ is time. Now, notice that,
$$
	\tau_{0} = \sqrt{ \frac{m}{k} }
$$
Has units of time. This is, in some sense, the natural time scale of this problem. We know from experience that this is the length of a single period.

If you try to find a natural length scale $l_{0}$ using the same methods, you will be unable to find one. So there is no natural length scale. Thus, the problem does not change if the length scale is altered. The oscillations do not look different if the mass is displaced by 1 or 2 metres.

---
b.
Notice that we have $kx$ which looks like $k$ times a length, and $ax^{3}$ looks like $a$ times a length cubed. Both of these are units of force. So we may naturally find,
$$
	\frac{k}{a} \sim  L^{2}
$$
Has a length squared attribute. Therefore,
$$
	\sqrt{ \frac{k}{a} } \sim L
$$
And this is our natural length scale $l_{0}$.

This tells us that the problem will probably change according to the amplitude.

Recall that energy has units,
$$
	E_{0} \sim  \frac{ml_{0}^{2}}{\tau_{0}^{2}} = \frac{k^{2}}{a}
$$
So we will have limiting cases according to this constant.
